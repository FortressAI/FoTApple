1. Automatic FRCP Deadline Calculation

100% Accuracy with Rule Citations

Federal Rules of Civil Procedure (FRCP) require precise deadline tracking. Missing a deadline can result in sanctions, dismissal, or malpractice claims. FoT Legal US automates this critical task with complete accuracy.

How It Works: 1. Enter trigger event (e.g., "Complaint filed") 2. System calculates all related deadlines per FRCP 3. Each deadline includes rule citation and calculation method 4. Reminders automatically set (configurable lead time) 5. Overdue deadlines highlighted in red

   Date: October 22, 2025
   Status: UPCOMING (21 days remaining)
   Rule: FRCP 12(a)(1)(A)(i)
   Calculation: 21 days after service of complaint
   Notes: Excludes weekends/holidays per FRCP 6(a)

   Date: November 21, 2025
   Status: PENDING

Example Analysis:

CONSTITUTIONAL ANALYSIS REQUEST

Question: Can Congress require all citizens to purchase health insurance?

ANALYSIS:

Relevant Constitutional Provisions:
1. Article I, Section 8, Clause 3 (Commerce Clause)
   "To regulate Commerce with foreign Nations, and among the 
   several States, and with the Indian Tribes"

2. Article I, Section 8, Clause 1 (Taxing Power)
   "The Congress shall have Power To lay and collect Taxes, Duties,
   Imposts and Excises..."

3. Tenth Amendment
   "The powers not delegated to the United States by the Constitution,
   nor prohibited by it to the States, are reserved to the States
   respectively, or to the people."

Supreme Court Precedent:

PRIMARY: National Federation of Independent Business v. Sebelius, 
         567 U.S. 519 (2012) ⭐ DIRECTLY ON POINT

Holding: Individual mandate constitutional as exercise of taxing power,
         but NOT under Commerce Clause.

Reasoning (Roberts, C.J.):
• Commerce Clause allows regulation of existing commerce, not 
  compulsion to enter commerce
• Penalty for not purchasing insurance = tax for constitutional purposes
• "Every reasonable construction must be resorted to, in order to save
  a statute from unconstitutionality"

Vote: 5-4 (Roberts, Ginsburg, Breyer, Sotomayor, Kagan)

RELATED PRECEDENT:

1. Wickard v. Filburn, 317 U.S. 111 (1942)
   → Broad Commerce Clause interpretation
   → Can regulate local activity with substantial effect on interstate commerce
   → Distinguishable: Regulating existing activity vs. compelling activity

2. United States v. Lopez, 514 U.S. 549 (1995)
   → Limits on Commerce Clause
   → Cannot regulate non-economic activity with attenuated commerce connection

3. South Dakota v. Dole, 483 U.S. 203 (1987)
   → Conditional federal funding constitutional
   → States not "coerced" when given choice

CONSTITUTIONAL CONCLUSION:

Under Taxing Power: ✅ LIKELY CONSTITUTIONAL
• NFIB v. Sebelius directly upheld individual mandate as tax
• Precedent remains good law
• Five-justice majority

Under Commerce Clause: ❌ LIKELY UNCONSTITUTIONAL
• NFIB rejected Commerce Clause justification
• Cannot compel citizens to enter commerce
• Exceeds enumerated powers

RECOMMENDED STRATEGY:
• Defend mandate as exercise of taxing power
• Cite NFIB extensively
• Distinguish from Commerce Clause cases (Lopez, Morrison)
• Emphasize deference to Congress on tax policy

Confidence: 95% (directly on-point SCOTUS precedent)
